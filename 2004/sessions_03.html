February 03, 2004:   Molecular Design and Synthesis

Presentation

Fraser Lecture (Part 1), 2/30/04
Fraser Lecture (Part 2), 2/30/04

Readings

LeCouteur and Burreson, Napoleon's Buttons: How 17 Molecules Changed History. Read the introduction and at least four chapters of interest to you.

Atkins, P. Atkins' Molecules, 2nd ed. Read the introduction and at least three chapters of interest to you.

Lab Book

Select three compounds of interest to you in Atkins' Molecules or from the websites below (e.g. Common Molecules Collection, Molecule of the Month.) Provide reasons for your selections. Draw the chemical structures of these compounds, using the simplified stick representation (e.g. Napoleon's Buttons, p. 14 bottom and p. 15). Practice other representations too, if you wish. You may need to look them up on the web or consult some other resources, including friends or classmates who are chemists.

Browse some of the following websites that show different representations of chemical structure. Make a descriptive list of the different kinds of representations that you find.

Questions

What are some other important molecules that would you add to the list of those that changed (or are changing) history?

In light of what you have learned this week, discuss and critique of the terms "natural", "organic", "synthetic", and "chemical-free" that often appear on labels. Are chemicals bad?

Important concepts: synthetic target, retrosynthetic analysis, synthetic strategy; relationship between composition, structure and reactivity; selectivity; isolation, purification, characterization; activity.
