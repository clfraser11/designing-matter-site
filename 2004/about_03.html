Common Courses Initiative

Designing Matter is the first of several Common Courses at Virginia supported by the "Teachers for a New Era" (TNE) grant, joint with the UVA Curry School of Education. (This program has its antecedents in the highly successful UVA Common Courses "21st Century Choices: War, Justice and Human Rights" headed by Professors James Childress and Michael Smith and "Environmental Decisions" also coordinated by Childress with an interdisciplinary team of faculty.) The Common Course program was envisioned by Ed Ayers, the Dean of Arts and Sciences, and is funded by support from the Carnegie Corporation, as well as the Annenberg and Ford Foundations. Victor Luftig (English) heads up the TNE grant for the Provost and the Teaching Resource Center Staff, Judith Reagan (Drama) with help from Dorothe Bach (German) and Marva Barnett (French), provides support for the Common Course component of it.

The grant seeks to improve universities' preparation of future K-12 teachers and emphasizes the involvement of Arts & Sciences faculty in that training. The Common Courses reflect this emphasis by seeing the multidisciplinary knowledge required of elementary school teachers as the model for all liberal arts training. In this way, Common Courses, with their sweep through the disciplines, address issues of content important for teachers in training, who ultimately might choose to enroll in this course. But they are also an innovative experiment in curriculum development for the College that aims to provide students with a shared experience, unified knowledge, and insights into how major branches of inquiry are interrelated across broad themes.

Four or five courses are envisioned, with their centers of gravity in the sciences, arts, humanities, and social sciences. Though like skin and leaf surfaces, these boundaries are not sealed, but have pores that are open. Like cell membranes, the division between inside and out is not fixed, but fluid. In this way, the Common Courses acknowledge and build upon longstanding traditions while incorporating into the curriculum what experience and scholarship are showing—that today's most fascinating questions and significant challenges often require new groupings of knowledge and expertise.

In Designing Matter and in other Common Courses, we will strive to understand the parts— large and small, loud and subtle, bright and dark, clear and barely perceptible—and the relationships between them from all manner of vantage points. We will track the movement back and forth across the boundaries, and thus, will come to better understand ourselves as designers and agents in this process.
