Graduate Teaching Assistants    >     Jennifer Aultman

Jennifer Aultman, currently a Ph.D. candidate in the Anthropology Department at U.Va., has pursued interdisciplinary topics since her early years as an undergraduate student at the University. After spending four years marching her way up and down McCormick Road, Jen received her B.A. from U.Va. with a double-major in Environmental Science and Archaeology, and a minor in Physics. Currently, her dissertation research in archaeology examines Monacan Indian settlement in Central Virginia from AD 800-1700. Specifically, she is interested in understanding the various ways that Monacan peoples shaped the landscapes in which they lived through the placement of their settlements, fields, and other built environments. Her work also explores the relationship between the "natural" landscape of the Rivanna River and its floodplain, and the "cultural" landscape built by Monacan people.

Additionally, Jen has expertise in the historical archaeology and social history of the Eastern U.S. She currently serves as Curator of Archaeological Collections at Monticello, a job that brings her in daily contact with the material remains of life in antebellum Virginia. She is particularly interested in considering how Monticello functioned as a working plantation, with a large and diverse enslaved community. Jen has also studied Plymouth Colony; her undergraduate Distinguished Major thesis examined the deterioration of relations between Native Americans and Colonists in Plymouth between 1620 and 1675.

Jen thoroughly enjoys teaching, and has served as Teaching Assistant for many courses at U.Va. She has also taught several courses of her own, including Intro to Archaeology (ANTH 280), Women's Lives in Myth and Reality (SWAG 210), and Field Methods in Archaeology (ANTH 381). In courses she teaches, Jen enjoys encouraging students to examine relationships between style, design, technology, function, social systems, and worldview. She is excited about discussing these topics in the Designing Matter course. Ultimately, she hopes to pursue a career in teaching because she firmly believes that we must consider alternative ways of seeing the world in order to realize how deeply our own cultural beliefs shape our experiences.
