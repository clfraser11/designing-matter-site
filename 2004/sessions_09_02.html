March 16, 2004:   Computer Graphics and Design Ethics

Part 1: Computer Graphics (page 1)
Part 2: Ethics and Technology

Presentation

Johnson Lecture, 3/16/04

Abstract

In this section we will consider the design of technology as a kind of designing matter. The first part will focus broadly on the relationship between technology and society considering how attention has shifted in the last two decades from an emphasis on how technology shapes society to understanding how a variety of social factors shape technology. The new emphasis has led some scholars to go as far as saying that technology is 'congealed culture.' [I'm wondering if the same might be said about matter.] Lessons learned about the social shaping of technology provide ideas that might be extended to understanding the design of matter.

The second segment will focus on the connection between ethics and technology. Here the emphasis will be on understanding that technology is not simply material objects; rather, technology is a combination of artifacts, social practices, social relationships, and systems of knowledge. Even if we focus on artifacts alone, they cannot be understood except as parts of socio-technical systems, and socio-technical systems definitely have moral features.

In the third and final section, we will focus on computers and information technology, as illustrations of the ideas from the first two parts. Both the question of whether the Internet is a democratic technology and, if time is available, other ethical issues surrounding computers and information technology will be considered.

Readings

T. J. Pinch and W. E. Bijker, "The Social Construction of Facts and Artifacts: Or How the Sociology of Science and the Sociology of Technology Might Benefit Each Other" from W. E. Bijker, T. P. Hughes, and T. Pinch, The Social Construction of Technological Systems. Cambridge: The MIT Press, 1987. (protected: part 1, part 2).

D.G. Johnson, "Is the Global Information Infrastructure a Democratic Technology?" Computers & Society 27 3 (1997): 20-26. (protected)

Questions

  • To what extent do scientific and technological developments occur independent of social forces?
  • Is technology (or matter) value-neutral?
  • Is the Internet a democratic technology?
  • Are computers or computer systems morally neutral?
