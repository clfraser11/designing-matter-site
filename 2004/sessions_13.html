April 13, 2004:   Politics of Climate Change

Presentation

Thompson Lecture, 4/13/04

Readings

"Climate Change Science: An Analysis of Some Key Questions" by the Committee on the Science of Climate Change, National Research Council, Washington, DC: National Academy Press, 2001. (protected)

European Parliament Directive 2003/87 on Greenhouse Gas Emission, I.275.82, Official Journal of the European Union, 25.10.2003. (protected)

"Five Letters to the President" Chapter 10 in Global Climate Change: The Science, Economics and Politics; James M. Griffin, Ed.; Cheltenham, UK; Northampton, MA: Edward Elgar, 2003. (protected)

Soroos, M. "Negotiating our Climate", Chapter 6 in Global Climate Change; Sharon L. Spray & Karen L. McGlothlin, Eds.; Lanham, MD: Rowman & Littlefield, 2002. (protected)

Victor, D. G. "International Agreements and the Struggle to Tame Carbon", Chapter 9 in Global Climate Change: The Science, Economics and Politics; James M. Griffin, Ed.; Cheltenham, UK; Northampton, MA: Edward Elgar, 2003. (protected)

Questions

  • What role should scientific information play in political decisions about climate change?
  • Why has the U. S. Senate thus far refused to ratify the Kyoto Protocol?
  • What was the effect of the Bush Administration's withdrawal from international climate change treaty negotiations?
  • Why has the European Union proceeded to implement the Kyoto Protocol, even though it is not officially in effect?
  • What is meant by "cap and trade" and why has this type of program been embraced in the Protocol?
  • Which of the letters to the President do you agree with?
  • How should we consider our obligations to future generations in our deliberations on climate change?
