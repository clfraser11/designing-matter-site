Speakers    >     Rosalyn Berne

Prof. Rosalyn Berne is currently an Assistant Professor of Technology, Culture, and Communication at the University of Virginia. She earned her B.A. and M.A. degrees in rhetoric and communication studies, and a Ph.D. in religious studies (bio-ethics). Her 15 years of employment at the University of Virginia have included positions as executive director of the Olsson Center of Applied Ethics and director of admissions at The Darden School, and University assistant vice president of administration. She also served for five years as head of Tandem Friends School in Charlottesville, Va.

Her published articles include: "Human-Machine Co-Evolution: Recognizing Religious Mythology in Visions of New Technology,"in IEEE Technology and Society Magazine; "Robosapiens, Transhumanism and the Kurzweilian Utopia: Why the Trans in Transhumanism," in IRIS; "Reaching and Teaching Through the Matrix"; "Ethics and the Formidable in Engineering Education," in the Proceedings of the ASEE Annual Meeting; "Reflections on Anencephalic Organ Use,"in BioLaw; and "Keeping Our Balance in the '90s: Women at Work, Women at Home," in Vital Speeches of the Day.Current professional memberships include the American Society for Engineering Education, The American Association for the Advancement of Science, The Foresight Institute (associate), and the Association for Practical and Professional Ethics.

Prof. Berne is currently focused directly on nanoscience and nanotechnology investigators, to understand the formulation of their personal motivations, beliefs, aspirations and goals, as well as the development of individual ethical frameworks, as these are connected to their research in nanotechnology. Her general research interests include engineering ethics, the use of technology for transhumanist aims, and the ethics of newly emerging technologies (i.e., nanotechnologies, artificial intelligence, robotics, genetic engineering). Prof. Berne uses narrative interpretation and rhetorical analysis of science fictional and non-fictional accounts of technology to understand the ethical frameworks of these developing technologies, as well as to consider how engineers and scientists formulate their own beliefs and values pertaining to our technological future.
